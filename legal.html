Legal

A summary of the key legislation and international standards that govern digital accessibility across different jurisdictions, with links to the original documents.

United States

Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) — 1990

The ADA is the primary civil rights law prohibiting discrimination against people with disabilities in the United States. It is divided into five titles, each covering a different sector. The most relevant to digital products is Title III, though Title II also has significant digital implications.

Title I — Employment
Prohibits discrimination against qualified individuals with disabilities in hiring, promotions, pay, and other employment practices. Requires employers with 15 or more employees to provide reasonable accommodations, which can include accessible workplace software and digital tools.
Title II — State and Local Government
Requires that all programs, services, and activities of state and local governments be accessible. This includes government websites, online portals, and digital services. The DOJ issued a final rule in 2024 establishing WCAG 2.1 Level AA as the technical standard for state and local government web content.
Title III — Public Accommodations
Requires businesses and organizations open to the public to be accessible. Courts and the DOJ have consistently interpreted this to include websites and mobile applications as "places of public accommodation." This is the title most commonly cited in digital accessibility litigation against private companies.
Title IV — Telecommunications
Requires telephone and television companies to provide accessible services, including relay services for deaf and hard-of-hearing users. Laid the groundwork for later legislation such as the CVAA.
Title V — Miscellaneous Provisions
Contains technical and legal provisions including anti-retaliation protections, the relationship between the ADA and other laws, and insurance provisions.

Who it applies to: Private businesses, non-profit organizations, and state/local governments that serve the public.

Standard required: No single technical standard is mandated in the original statute. WCAG 2.1 Level AA is the accepted benchmark used by courts and the DOJ; the 2024 rule formalises this for Title II entities.

  • ADA.gov — Official ADA website (opens in a new tab)
  • DOJ Guidance on Web Accessibility and the ADA (opens in a new tab)
  • ADA Title II 2024 Final Rule — Web and Mobile Accessibility (opens in a new tab)

Section 508 of the Rehabilitation Act — 1998 (amended 2017)

Section 508 requires that federal agencies make their electronic and information technology accessible to people with disabilities. The 2017 refresh aligned Section 508 with WCAG 2.0 Level AA and extended its scope to include software, websites, electronic documents, and hardware. It applies to federal agencies developing, procuring, maintaining, or using ICT, as well as organizations receiving federal funding.

Who it applies to: US federal agencies and organizations that receive federal contracts or funding.

Standard required: WCAG 2.0 Level AA (incorporated by reference).

  • Section508.gov — Official resource center (opens in a new tab)
  • US Access Board — ICT Accessibility Standards (opens in a new tab)

21st Century Communications and Video Accessibility Act (CVAA) — 2010

The CVAA modernised federal communications law to reflect advancements in digital, broadband, and mobile technologies. It requires that internet browsers on mobile phones, video programming (including streaming), and communications services be accessible to people with disabilities.

Who it applies to: Telecommunications and video programming providers, device manufacturers.

  • FCC — CVAA Overview (opens in a new tab)

European Union

Web Accessibility Directive — 2016

Directive (EU) 2016/2102 requires that websites and mobile applications of public sector bodies in EU member states meet accessibility standards. All public sector websites must publish an accessibility statement and provide a feedback mechanism. Member states must monitor compliance and report to the European Commission.

Who it applies to: Public sector bodies in EU member states (governments, public authorities, publicly funded organizations).

Standard required: EN 301 549, which incorporates WCAG 2.1 Level AA.

  • Directive (EU) 2016/2102 — Official text (opens in a new tab)

European Accessibility Act (EAA) — 2019 (effective June 2025)

Directive (EU) 2019/882 extends accessibility requirements to the private sector. It covers a wide range of products and services including e-commerce websites and apps, banking services, e-books, audiovisual media services, and consumer hardware. Member states were required to transpose the directive into national law by June 2022, with enforcement obligations applying from 28 June 2025.

Who it applies to: Private sector businesses operating in the EU that provide covered products or services — including non-EU businesses that sell into the EU market.

Standard required: EN 301 549 / WCAG 2.1 Level AA for digital products and services.

  • Directive (EU) 2019/882 — Official text (opens in a new tab)
  • European Commission — European Accessibility Act (opens in a new tab)

United Kingdom

Equality Act 2010

The Equality Act protects people from discrimination based on protected characteristics, including disability. It requires service providers (including those offering services online) to make "reasonable adjustments" to avoid placing disabled people at a substantial disadvantage. An inaccessible website may constitute unlawful discrimination under this act.

Who it applies to: Any organization providing services to the public in Great Britain, including websites and apps.

  • Equality Act 2010 — legislation.gov.uk (opens in a new tab)

Public Sector Bodies Accessibility Regulations 2018 (PSBAR)

These regulations implement the EU Web Accessibility Directive into UK law and were retained after Brexit. They require public sector websites and mobile apps to meet WCAG 2.1 Level AA and publish an accessibility statement. The regulations are monitored by the Cabinet Office.

Who it applies to: UK public sector bodies, including central and local government, NHS, universities, and other publicly funded organizations.

Standard required: WCAG 2.1 Level AA.

  • Public Sector Bodies Accessibility Regulations 2018 — legislation.gov.uk (opens in a new tab)
  • GOV.UK — Accessibility requirements for public sector websites and apps (opens in a new tab)

Canada

Accessibility for Ontarians with Disabilities Act (AODA) — 2005

AODA requires organizations in Ontario to meet accessibility standards across customer service, employment, information and communications, transportation, and the built environment. The Integrated Accessibility Standards Regulation (IASR) requires that websites and web content conform to WCAG 2.0 Level AA. Large organizations (50+ employees) and public sector bodies have been required to comply since 2014–2021 depending on organization size.

Who it applies to: Public and private sector organizations in Ontario with one or more employees.

Standard required: WCAG 2.0 Level AA.

  • AODA — Ontario legislation (opens in a new tab)
  • Ontario.ca — About accessibility laws (opens in a new tab)

Accessible Canada Act (ACA) — 2019

The ACA aims to create a barrier-free Canada by 2040. It requires federally regulated organizations — including banks, telecommunications companies, broadcasters, and federal government entities — to identify, remove, and prevent barriers to accessibility across all areas including information and communication technology. Regulated organizations must publish and implement accessibility plans and progress reports.

Who it applies to: Federal public sector and federally regulated private sector organizations (banks, telecoms, broadcasters, transportation).

  • Accessible Canada Act — Justice Laws Website (opens in a new tab)
  • Government of Canada — Accessible Canada (opens in a new tab)

Australia

Disability Discrimination Act 1992 (DDA)

The DDA makes it unlawful to discriminate against a person on the grounds of disability. The Australian Human Rights Commission has consistently held that an inaccessible website constitutes discrimination under the DDA. While the Act does not mandate a specific technical standard, the Commission's advisory notes reference WCAG as the appropriate benchmark. Public sector bodies are additionally bound by the Web Accessibility National Transition Strategy.

Who it applies to: All organizations providing services or goods to the public in Australia.

Standard referenced: WCAG 2.0 Level AA (advisory).

  • Disability Discrimination Act 1992 — Federal Register of Legislation (opens in a new tab)
  • AHRC — World Wide Web Access: DDA Advisory Notes (opens in a new tab)

International Standards

Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG)

WCAG is a set of technical guidelines published by the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C) under its Web Accessibility Initiative (WAI). While not legislation itself, WCAG is the technical standard referenced by virtually every accessibility law worldwide. The current version is WCAG 2.2 (published October 2023). WCAG 3.0 is in active development. Most laws reference WCAG 2.0 or 2.1 Level AA as the compliance benchmark.

  • WCAG 2.2 — W3C Recommendation (opens in a new tab)
  • W3C WAI — WCAG Overview (opens in a new tab)

UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD) — 2006

The CRPD is an international human rights treaty adopted by the United Nations General Assembly. Article 9 explicitly requires that signatories ensure equal access for persons with disabilities to information and communications technology, including the internet. Over 180 countries have ratified the convention, making it the broadest legal framework for disability rights globally. While not directly enforceable in domestic courts, ratifying states are obligated to align national laws with its principles.

  • UN — Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (opens in a new tab)
  • OHCHR — CRPD full text (opens in a new tab)

EN 301 549 — European Standard for ICT Accessibility

EN 301 549 is a European harmonised standard that specifies accessibility requirements for ICT products and services, including websites, software, and hardware. It incorporates WCAG 2.1 Level AA for web content and is the standard referenced by the EU Web Accessibility Directive and European Accessibility Act. It is produced by ETSI, CEN, and CENELEC.

  • EN 301 549 v3.2.1 — ETSI PDF (opens in a new tab)
